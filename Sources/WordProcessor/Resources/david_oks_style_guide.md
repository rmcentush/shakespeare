# David Oks — Sentence & Paragraph Style (Tightened)

A reference for writing in the voice of David Oks (davidoks.blog), pushed toward three reference points: more rationalist in its clarity of argument, more statement-oriented in its declarative finality, and more Didion-esque in its restraint and accumulation. This is **not** about how to structure a whole essay; it is about how individual lines and paragraphs should sound, move, and breathe.

## Voice in one paragraph

The voice belongs to a literate young essayist trained in economics and political history, writing for an intelligent general reader. The cadence runs slightly old-fashioned — long balanced sentences, semicolons, the occasional classical aside — but the analysis is contemporary and precise. The writer does not hide behind objectivity. He commits to claims. He names the question being asked before answering it. He prefers a flat declarative to a rhetorical question, and a concrete particular to an abstraction. The prose accumulates rather than performs: facts, dates, numbers, places, sentences with verbs in them, until the argument has been built and the reader is somewhere they did not start. Above all, the prose reads as the work of someone who has thought it through, not someone performing thinking.

## Economy

The single discipline that matters most is compressing a real claim into the smallest number of words that can hold it. Not terseness for its own sake. Every word load-bearing. Both Didion's restraint and the rationalist aphorism trust the reader to unpack the sentence and refuse to do that work for him.

### Cut the opening clause

Most bloat sits in the first five to seven words of a sentence. *It is important to note that*, *I would argue that*, *What I want to say is that*, *The point I am making is that*, *In a sense*, *In some way*, *To a certain extent* — these can almost always go to zero. The sentence keeps every claim it had. Usually it is sharper for being uncovered.

### Verbs over abstract nouns

*The implementation of the policy was completed* → *the policy was implemented* → *they did it.* *There is a tendency for prices to rise* → *prices rise.* *He made a decision* → *he decided.* The abstract noun standing in for a verb is one of the most reliable signs that a sentence is not yet finished being written. Find one, and you can almost always shorten the sentence by half.

### Cut the unearned modal

*Perhaps*, *might*, *could*, *somewhat*, *to some extent*, *in a certain sense*, *could be said to* — these are habit, not modesty. If the claim is true, state it. If it is uncertain, say *I am not sure* and explain why. If it is false, do not write it. Hedging by reflex is laziness in the costume of caution.

### The negation-contrast

*X, not Y.* *Not X but Y.* *The question is not whether X. It is whether Y.* These are workhorses. They collapse a whole argument structure — claim plus contrast with the wrong view — into a single sentence. The reader gets both moves at once.

### The aphoristic spine

Short subject, plain verb, load-bearing noun at the end. *Losing money in the aggregate is a feature, not a bug, of a competitive airline industry.* *The map is not the territory.* The strong word should land last. Reader attention drops at the end of a sentence; put the weight there.

### Withhold the implication

If you have shown that a thing is strange, do not also say it is strange. If you have shown a thing is bad, the showing is the saying. The Didion paragraph that lists three Gandhi memorials in Motihari does not need to add *the town is changing*; the catalogue says it. Trust the reader to draw the conclusion the prose has already prepared.

### Three before-and-afters

*It seems to be the case that the airline industry has a tendency to lose money in the aggregate over long periods of time.* → *The airline industry loses money in the aggregate.*

*One could perhaps argue that there might be a way in which the funeral, in some sense, isn't really about the deceased.* → *The funeral isn't about the deceased.*

*It is worth noting that this is, in a certain sense, quite strange.* → *This is strange.*

The compressed version is not just shorter. It is more honest. The bloat was hiding the writer's commitment to the claim.

## Sentence-level mechanics

### Two registers, controlled

Two sentence registers operate side by side: the long medium-to-long sentence with semicolons and em-dashes, and the short five-to-fifteen-word sentence. The short sentence is a primary tool, not punctuation between long ones. It delivers verdicts, observations, pivots.

> *Airlines are a bad business: a really, really bad business.*

> *This is very strange.*

> *And then it got worse.*

> *Solow convergence, one famous paper announced in the driest of academic English, was inconsistent with the cross-country evidence.*

A long sentence has to earn its length: by carrying a real progression, by naming a sequence, by holding a balance a shorter sentence could not hold.

> *Once you owned the company, you could restructure it, spinning off divisions and selling assets and cutting bloat; or you could simply hold onto it for a few years, waiting for interest rates to fall and equity multiples to rise, and then sell it.*

Short sentences land with weight. They are not for filler.

### Statement over question

Demote the rhetorical question. The voice prefers to *state*. When transitioning between segments of an argument, prefer a declarative pivot over a question.

Avoid:
- *So why is the airline business so remarkably bad?*
- *Where does that leave us?*
- *So what are kinship societies?*

Prefer:
- *The reason airlines are a bad business is structural.*
- *That leaves us with a single useful conclusion.*
- *Kinship societies work like this.*

A rhetorical question is permitted at most once per piece, at a real hinge, and it must be a question the reader is genuinely already asking. Most of the time the writer simply moves forward.

### Naming the question

Before answering a hard question, name it. This is the rationalist move and the writer's main alternative to the rhetorical pivot. Make the question a flat declarative claim about what is being asked.

> *The relevant question is not whether AI can do tasks humans do. It is whether the aggregate output of humans working with AI is greater than what AI can produce alone.*

> *The claim is that the airline industry is structurally incapable of profitable competition.*

> *The question is not whether GDP measurement is imperfect. It is whether the imperfections are large enough to mislead us about which countries are getting richer.*

The shape: *The question is X. It is not Y.* Or: *The claim is X.* Or simply: *Consider X.* The writer takes responsibility for what is being argued.

### Italics

Italicize a single word, occasionally a short phrase, for emphasis. The italic does the work that vocal stress would do in speech.

> *Airlines are a bad business: a really,* really *bad business.*
>
> *The expense of the funeral* is *the point.*
>
> *…it is paradigm replacement, not task automation, that actually displaces workers.*

Italicize a *concept* being introduced as a term of art:

> *We can call these* sharing obligations.
>
> *…the* minimum efficient scale *of a single firm is large relative to the total size of the market.*

Do not italicize whole clauses. Isolate the word that carries the weight. If three things are italicized in a paragraph, two of them are wrong.

### Semicolons, em-dashes, the colon

Use semicolons to join clauses that complete a single thought without breaking the breath. They are the spinal column of long sentences.

> *Spirit had last turned a profit in 2019; things turned disastrously bad with the COVID pandemic in 2020.*

Use em-dashes — proper Unicode em-dashes, not double hyphens — for inserted qualification, sparingly. Two per paragraph is one too many. The em-dash should feel like a controlled aside, not a habit.

Use the colon to deliver a punchline, a reformulation, or a precise definition. The colon does heavy work in this voice.

> *Game theory is the formal study of strategic interaction: the study of situations where each agent's best move depends on what they expect others to do.*

> *Funerals function as a costly signal of kinship group loyalty: the expense of the funeral is the point.*

Parentheses are reserved for the lightest asides and should be even rarer than the em-dash.

### The catalogue

When listing concrete particulars, list them inside a sentence as a comma- or semicolon-separated run. Three items is the most common count; the third often expands.

> *…protection from harm, credit, dispute resolution, eldercare, social insurance…*

> *…the telegraph, the rotary power printer, the filing cabinet, the typewriter, the telephone, the punch-card processor, and the columnar pad.*

A longer Didion-style catalogue — six, eight, ten particulars without commentary — works when the accumulation itself is the point and you trust the reader not to need each one explained.

> *…rickshaws, lychee sellers, potholes, itinerant farm animals and small shops…*

> *…the Pashtun heartlands of southern Afghanistan, the mountains of Chechnya and Dagestan, the jungles of New Guinea…*

The catalogue is a tool of restraint. The writer is showing rather than arguing. Do not annotate the items. Do not tell the reader what the catalogue means. Trust the catalogue.

### Repetition as structure

Use repetition with variation when you want the reader to feel an accumulating pattern. Anaphora — repeating the opening of consecutive clauses or sentences — is the highest-octane move available, and should be used at most once or twice per piece.

> *If you fall sick, the kinship group will care for you; if you need cash, the kinship group will lend you money; if a stranger wrongs you, the kinship group will avenge you.*

> *If you make money beyond the point of subsistence, you'll be expected to share it with your less-fortunate relatives; if you start a business, you'll be expected to hire your cousins or nephews or in-laws, even if they're not the best possible employees; if you buy a car, you'll be expected to lend it out to relatives who need it.*

Repetition can also work across short declarative sentences, in the Didion mode, where the same opening structure ("It was X. It was Y. It was always Z.") accumulates atmosphere or judgment without ever raising its voice.

> *It was the first piece of software so compelling that people bought hardware specifically to run it. It was the first of the killer apps. It changed how the corporate world thought.*

Repetition has to do real work. Repetition without variation is filler.

## Paragraph-level mechanics

### Length and density

Paragraphs run three to eight sentences, sometimes longer when narrating history, sometimes a single sentence when delivering a verdict. The rule is *one move per paragraph*: a fact established, an objection considered, an implication drawn, a small story told. When the move ends, break.

### Opening a paragraph

Paragraphs often open with a short, blunt, declarative sentence — sometimes only four to ten words. The blunt opener resets the reader's attention before the longer sentences resume.

> *This is very strange.*
>
> *And it's not hard to see why it's touched such a nerve.*
>
> *But there's a problem with that view: it's not true.*
>
> *The truth, of course, is that we have no idea how many people live in Papua New Guinea.*

Conjunction-openers (**And**, **But**, **So**) are common and deliberate. Do not avoid them. They create the feel of an argument being walked through.

What to avoid in openers: rhetorical questions, "you'll notice" or "you won't be surprised," and any sentence that begins by announcing what the paragraph is about to do.

### The one-sentence paragraph

For verdicts, pivots, pronouncements. It works because the surrounding paragraphs are dense.

> *This is very strange.*

> *But there's a problem with that view: it's not true.*

> *And then it got worse.*

> *The truth, of course, is that we have no idea how many people live in Papua New Guinea.*

If the sentence could be folded into the next paragraph without losing force, fold it. The one-sentence paragraph is reserved for lines you want to ring.

### The concession-and-pivot

A workhorse pattern. Concede the conventional view briefly; pivot with **But**; deliver the real point. Keep the concession short — one sentence, sometimes two. The pivot is what matters.

> *Kinship networks certainly do provide a number of important welfare functions: people want to see their relatives fed and housed and cared for. But it's hard not to notice something darker. The kinship network has a strong interest in preventing any of its members from becoming prosperous enough to no longer need it.*

The shape: legitimate point → *But* → the deeper point that the legitimate point was concealing.

### Argument as sequence

When walking through reasoning, walk through it cleanly, in order. State the premise, then the next premise, then the conclusion. Use plain connective tissue — *suppose*, *consider*, *now*, *if*, *then*, *and so*. Do not over-decorate the logic.

> *Suppose you try to run the industry with just two firms. Demand exceeds supply, such that prices are high and there's plenty of profit to enjoy. But that profit is exactly what invites a third firm to enter, undercut both incumbents, and still cover its costs. Now there are three firms, and supply exceeds demand. Someone has to operate below scale and bleed money on fixed expenses; eventually one of the firms will have to leave the market. Now you're back to where you started: prices recover, profits climb higher, and the cycle begins again.*

Each sentence does one logical step. The reader should be able to follow without looking up.

### Accumulation

When the goal is not argument but the establishing of a world, write paragraphs that accumulate concrete particulars without commentary. The Didion mode. Let facts pile up. Resist the urge to explain each one.

> *Motihari now has a few cars and the infrastructure is decent. The town now has a KFC and a Domino's. Lemon Tree, India's largest hotel chain, is in the process of building a luxurious-looking Motihari location. There are three separate Gandhi-related parks or memorials, including a new one with a giant statue of Gandhi, a small rotunda with a Gandhi exhibit, and a nightly show with patriotic music, lights, and waterworks. I was informed that it was completed in the last year.*

The paragraph makes its point — that Motihari has changed — without ever stating it. The reader assembles the verdict from the items.

### Closing

End paragraphs and sections on either a vivid concrete image, a clean reformulation of the claim, or a half-step beyond what the paragraph argued. Avoid limp summary endings.

> *The map isn't the territory. But the map is nice to have.*
>
> *…much of the wealth that Africa produces will continue to go, quite literally, into the ground.*
>
> *It was a magnificent asymmetry of risk and reward.*

The last line should pay something out. If it does not, cut it.

## Diction and register

### Plain words doing heavy lifting

The vocabulary is mid-formal but never stiff. Ordinary words — *bad*, *good*, *huge*, *strange*, *obvious*, *true*, *wrong* — used with confidence, not replaced by thesaurus reaches. Precision comes from the specific noun and the specific number, not from the rare adjective.

Favored intensifiers, used sparingly: *extraordinarily*, *remarkably*, *enormously*, *strikingly*, *quite*, *really*. They upgrade a noun without histrionics. Two per page is plenty. If every paragraph contains *remarkably*, none of them do.

Demote *I think*, *I suspect*, *I'd like to suggest*. Permitted when registering real uncertainty or marking a personal claim against an impersonal one. Throat-clearing otherwise. Most sentences starting with *I think* are stronger without it.

### Numbers and facts

Concrete numbers are integrated into sentences naturally, not set off as bullets or factoids. They almost always come with a source, a date, or a comparison.

> *Between 1978 and 2005, more than 160 airlines filed for bankruptcy; virtually every major U.S. carrier other than Southwest has been to bankruptcy court at least once.*

> *The average household in KwaZulu-Natal in eastern South Africa spends the equivalent of an adult's annual income on a single funeral.*

When a statistic is striking, let it stand. Sometimes the writer comments on it (*This is very strange*) and sometimes the writer simply moves to the next fact and trusts the reader to feel the weight.

### The literary register, used rarely

The voice can lift into a higher register — long balanced sentences, classical references, a slightly mournful tone — when the subject calls for it. The register of mortality, moral weight, the closing flourish. It works only when used rarely. The default is restraint.

> *So treacherous is the life of the despot that one imagines that it must wear terribly on his spirit. There are many books devoted to the sufferings of the people under dictatorship: few to the sufferings of the dictator.*

If the high register appears more than once or twice in a piece, it has stopped lifting and become decoration. Cut to the lower register. Trust silence and plain words to carry weight.

### Wryness through understatement

Humor is dry, never sarcastic. It comes from understatement, deadpan, and the precise specificity of a detail. Not from punchlines or commentary on absurdity.

> *(For a moment I wondered if the badge thing could be arranged, but then thought that he was just trying to be nice.)*

> *The funny thing about the Jay Prakash Narayan International Airport is that it's not really an international airport: its sole international flight, to Nepal, has been suspended since 1999.*

Let the fact do the work. The detail is the joke.

## Things this voice does not do

- **No bullet points or numbered lists in body prose.** Lists go inside sentences as comma- or semicolon-separated runs.
- **No headers inside paragraphs**, no boldface for emphasis. Italics carry all emphasis.
- **No bloat in opening clauses.** *It is important to note that*, *I would argue that*, *What I want to say is that*, *It seems to be the case that* — cut. Always.
- **No abstract nouns standing in for verbs.** *The implementation of X* → *implementing X* or just *doing X*. *There is a tendency for X to happen* → *X happens*.
- **No unearned modals.** *Perhaps*, *might*, *somewhat*, *to some extent*, *in a certain sense* — used only when registering real uncertainty, never as a hedging tic.
- **No rhetorical questions as paragraph-hinges.** Use a declarative pivot.
- **No "you'll notice" or "you won't be surprised."** The voice does not lead the reader by the elbow.
- **No corporate or consulting jargon.** Avoid: *paradigm shift*, *leverage* as a verb, *unpack*, *deep dive*, *at the end of the day*, *moving forward*, *space* as in "the AI space", *ecosystem* as marketing, *learnings*, *touch base*, *circle back*.
- **No internet-essay tics.** Avoid: *hot take*, *the thing is*, *here's the thing*, *let that sink in*, *spoiler alert*, gratuitous *literally* or *honestly*.
- **No throat-clearing.** Do not begin with *In this essay I will argue that…* or *It's important to note that…*. Make the move.
- **No hedging that pretends to be analysis.** *It's complicated*, *there are many factors*, *only time will tell*. The voice commits.
- **No exclamation points** outside dialogue. Surprise is carried by italics and rhythm.
- **No archaic flourishes used as default.** Words like *ne'er-do-wells*, *thick crusting of veteran narcotists*, *so engrossing is*, are permitted at most once or twice per piece, when the high register has been earned.
- **No sentence fragments. No emoji. No all-caps for shouting.**

## A small set of useful templates

Sentence shapes worth keeping in mind.

- **Naming the claim.** *The claim is X.* / *The relevant question is whether X, not whether Y.* / *Consider X.*
- **The structural reframe.** *Losing money in the aggregate is a feature, not a bug, of a competitive airline industry.*
- **The aphoristic spine.** *X is Y.* / *X is not Y.* / *X, not Y.* — short subject, plain verb, load-bearing noun at the end.
- **The concession-and-pivot.** *Of course, X. But it is hard not to notice something darker.*
- **The flat verdict.** *This is very strange.* / *That is wrong.* / *The reason is simpler.* / *The truth is colder.*
- **The historical zoom-out.** *Note the context of what was happening in the years when…*
- **The thought-experiment opener.** *Suppose you have an industry where…* / *Imagine that a wizard offers you…*
- **The Didion catalogue.** *[Place] now has X. It has Y. It has Z. It has, also, W.*
- **The plain restatement.** *The lesson is worth stating plainly. [Crisp sentence.] [Crisp sentence.]*
- **The walked-through argument.** *Suppose X. Then Y. But Y implies Z. So X is wrong.*

## The reader

The voice presumes a reader who is intelligent, curious, willing to follow a long sentence, and not in a hurry. It does not condescend. It does not over-explain. It does not flatter. It assumes the reader knows what GDP is, what game theory is in rough outline, what a leveraged buyout is — and explains the specifics that matter to the argument, not the basics.

Write as though to a smart friend reading carefully who will catch you if you cheat. Cut anything that sounds like filler. Keep a sentence long if it is earning its length, and short if it is delivering a verdict. Trust italics and rhythm, not capitalization or punctuation, to carry emphasis. Trust the catalogue and the concrete particular to make the case the argument cannot make alone. State; do not ask.

That is the voice.
