# Writing quality baseline

Use this as a fallback for drafting and editing, not as an AI detector or a
list of forbidden words. A pattern matters when it is unearned, repetitive, or
stacked with other patterns. The writer's explicit request, reviewed style
profile, deliberate choices in the draft, and representative samples override
these defaults. Fix the underlying weakness; do not merely disguise a tell.

## Core anti-pattern check

- Lead with the answer, action, image, or concrete claim. Cut prompt restatement,
  throat-clearing, meta-narration, chatbot pleasantries, and previews of what the
  prose is about to do.
- Prefer plain, specific statements to inflated importance, promotional praise,
  vague authority, generic emotion, or claims that something is historic,
  transformative, notable, or surprising without evidence.
- Break formulaic symmetry when it adds no meaning: repeated “not X but Y”
  pivots, automatic groups of three, rhetorical-question transitions, teaser
  fragments, uniform sentence or paragraph lengths, and canned recaps.
- Use the clearest word consistently. Do not cycle synonyms or replace “is” and
  “has” with press-release verbs merely to sound polished.
- Use headings, bullets, bold, transitions, and dashes only when the content
  needs them. No punctuation mark or construction is banned; preserve a habit
  when the writer's profile or the passage shows it is intentional.

## Substance and evidence

- Preserve facts, names, numbers, quotations, citations, causal direction, and
  necessary uncertainty. Never invent specificity to make prose feel concrete.
- Name the source behind an attributed claim. Replace “experts say,” “research
  shows,” or “critics argue” with a checkable attribution, or remove the appeal
  to authority.
- State the actual consequence instead of announcing significance. Do not add a
  broad social lesson, legacy claim, moral, future outlook, or balanced
  “challenges and opportunities” ending that the evidence did not earn.
- Keep useful qualifications, but remove stacked hedges and empty confidence
  cues such as “it is important to note,” “notably,” or “without a doubt.”

## Language and rhythm

- Prefer precise nouns, ordinary verbs, and the writer's established register.
  Avoid corporate filler, abstract noun piles, clichés, and ornamental language
  that does not sharpen meaning.
- Let sentence length and syntax follow the thought. Preserve effective
  fragments, repetition, contractions, informality, and uneven pacing; do not
  manufacture randomness or sand away every irregularity.
- Use a real logical transition when cause, contrast, chronology, or
  qualification needs one. Cut serial “Additionally,” “Moreover,” “Furthermore,”
  “Let's explore,” “Here's the thing,” and “The catch?” scaffolding.
- Use contrast only when both sides matter. Do not invent a misconception so the
  sentence can correct it, and do not pad a claim into a compulsory trio.

## Structure and endings

- Give each paragraph a distinct job and make it advance the surrounding
  argument. Remove repeated setup, conclusions already established, and opening
  sentences that merely recap the previous section.
- Use lists for genuinely list-shaped information and headings for real changes
  of subject. Avoid decorative micro-sections, parallel mini-essays, and generic
  headings such as “Overview,” “Key takeaways,” or “Conclusion.”
- End on the passage's actual consequence, decision, evidence, or image. Do not
  append a generic summary, invitation to continue, or “only time will tell.”

## Revision discipline

- Make the smallest change that fixes a real problem. If the passage already
  works, leave it alone.
- Match perspective, stance, contraction rate, punctuation, vocabulary,
  sentence-length pattern, paragraph movement, and level of detail to the
  reviewed profile and samples. Never copy their topic, facts, or distinctive
  phrases, and never imitate a named writer.
- Read the result once more for stacked habits and overcorrection. A clean edit
  should sound like this writer at their best, not like a generic “humanized”
  persona.

## Selection feedback

- Start with the highest-value assessment of the selected passage, then give no
  more than three specific points. Distinguish a real weakness from a personal
  preference and name effective choices worth preserving.
- Diagnose before demonstrating. Use a small local alternative only when it
  makes the advice clearer; do not replace the entire selection by default.

## Gap completion

- Write the missing prose rather than discussing the gap. Make it connect to
  both sides, serve the section's purpose, and match the surrounding level of
  detail without restating an established point.
- Prefer the shortest complete fill that satisfies the writer's note. If facts
  are missing, use a neutral bridge instead of inventing names, claims, numbers,
  quotations, or citations.

## Ambient review

- Surface only high-signal issues that justify interrupting the writer. Prefer
  silence to low-confidence polish, and never repeat an existing comment.
- Keep each suggestion locally actionable and safely anchored. Consider the
  paragraph's role in the full document before changing its opening, ending,
  transition, or rhythm.
